# Dynamic Pricing EDA and Feature Engineering Workflow

## Executive Summary

Dynamic pricing EDA is not generic data profiling. In pricing, price or discount is a decision variable, usually endogenous, and the point of EDA is to answer four hard questions before modeling: can I trust the logs, do I have enough variation and overlap to learn price response, what confounders are driving historical price assignment, and is the demand process stable enough over time to support deployment. The demand-estimation literature treats this as a core identification problem, not just a prediction problem, and the forecasting literature makes the complementary point that temporal structure, residual diagnostics, and cross-validation must respect time order. That combination turns the workflow into a hybrid of predictive modeling, causal inference, and policy evaluation. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn0file0Ņłü ŅłĆciteŅłéturn4search8Ņłéturn24search0Ņłü

In practice, the workflow I would follow as a staff Data Scientist is: establish the causal and temporal validity of the data; engineer features that encode price state, customer or item history, constraints, seasonality, and exogenous shocks; choose the simplest model family consistent with the EDA; and evaluate not just predictive error but decision quality, calibration, constraint compliance, and offline policy value. If the data are approximately log-linear and you need interpretable elasticities, I stay close to GLMs and regularized linear models; if the response is highly nonlinear and interaction-heavy, I move to tree ensembles; if treatment assignment is confounded and action optimization is the real objective, I layer in doubly robust or uplift-style estimation and, eventually, online experiments or contextual bandits. ## Problem Framing and Assumptions

Because your prompt asks for a workflow independent of a specific dataset, I assume a repeated-observations pricing panel at a business grain such as user-session, SKU-day-market, or request-zone-minute. I also assume that the action is chosen by the business rather than randomized, that outcomes are often zero-inflated and right-skewed, that repeated customers or items create temporal dependence, and that deployment faces business rules such as price floors, margin targets, or minimum action coverage. Those assumptions match both modern pricing practice and the attached assessmentŌĆÖs logged-offer setup. ŅłĆfileciteŅłéturn0file0Ņłü ŅłĆciteŅłéturn20search6Ņłéturn24search0Ņłéturn18search10Ņłü

The target should be framed as a decision target, not only a forecast target. Let \(x\) denote context, \(a\) the price or discount action, \(q\) the purchased quantity, and \(r\) the reward. Then the policy problem is to choose \(a\) to maximize \(E[r \mid x, a]\), subject to business constraints. In many pricing systems I model this either directly, with a revenue or contribution target, or as a decomposition:
\[
E[r \mid x,a] = P(\text{purchase}=1 \mid x,a)\times E[q \mid x,a,\text{purchase}=1]\times \text{unit price}(a),
\]
because conversion and conditional quantity often have different distributions and drivers. For non-negative long-tailed outcomes, Poisson and Tweedie-style models or transformed-target regression become natural options. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn6search2Ņłéturn6search3Ņłéturn6search5Ņłéturn3search3Ņłü

One assumption must be handled explicitly: historical price assignment is usually confounded. The industrial-organization demand literature treats price as endogenous; in modern ML language, the treatment mechanism must be modeled because a purely predictive model may learn who historically received discounts rather than the incremental effect of a discount. That is why I treat ŌĆ£who got which price?ŌĆØ as a first-class modeling object, usually through balance tables, propensity models, overlap checks, and eventually doubly robust or causal-forest style estimators when I need counterfactual price response rather than raw forecasting. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn20search6Ņłéturn30search0Ņłéturn1search2Ņłéturn1search7Ņłü

The final assumption is about interference. If selling one unit at a given price changes inventory, wait times, driver availability, or competitor behavior, then a na├»ve unit-level model can misprice the system. AmazonŌĆÖs published work on price experimentation highlights spillovers and randomization design, while ride-hailing examples emphasize that price is also used to balance supply and demand. So a staff-level workflow explicitly asks whether the problem is partial-equilibrium personalization, system-level marketplace control, or something in between. ## Feature Engineering Playbook

Good pricing features encode five things at once: current context, recent history, price state, market structure, and deployment legality. The forecasting literature is especially useful for temporal and hierarchical features, while the pricing and causal literature is useful for price-state and treatment-response features. The central staff-level principle is that every feature must be available at decision time and must have a clear ŌĆ£as-ofŌĆØ boundary. ## How EDA Should Change Modeling Decisions

If I do a full EDA and still say ŌĆ£we can try linear, trees, causal forests, ARIMA errors, or a bandit,ŌĆØ then I have not actually learned enough from the data. The better framing is: which failure modes dominate, and which model family is best suited to that failure mode while staying interpretable and deployable enough for pricing? A few specific EDA-to-model mappings are especially important in pricing. If raw price-response is noisy but partial regression plots become roughly linear after conditioning on seasonality, supply, and customer state, that usually argues for a regularized linear or GLM backbone, sometimes with spline bases for only the obviously nonlinear variables. If partial plots still show curvature and interaction-heavy behavior, trees become the default. If you can articulate a monotone effect such as ŌĆ£conversion probability should not increase with price, holding everything else fixed,ŌĆØ then monotonic constraints are often worth using. I apply those constraints on demand or conversion models more often than on direct revenue models, because expected revenue can legitimately be hump-shaped in price. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn21search3Ņłéturn21search8Ņłéturn7search4Ņłéturn7search6Ņłéturn7search9Ņłü

Regularization is not optional once the feature set includes interactions, segments, or cross-item terms. Elastic Net is the usual starting point for correlated tabular features, and hierarchical shrinkage becomes attractive when cross-price structure grows large and you still want interpretable elasticities. That last point matters in real pricing systems: the number of possible substitution effects grows very quickly, so shrinkage is not just a statistical nicety but a feasibility requirement. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn6search0Ņłéturn28search1Ņłéturn28search12Ņłü

Sample weighting should follow the business and identification problem. For heteroscedastic regression with known variance structure, WLS uses weights proportional to inverse variance. For observational policy evaluation, inverse propensity or doubly robust corrections matter more than business weights. And if the business objective emphasizes certain transactions, markets, or margin buckets, then fit-time and score-time weighting need to be routed consistently through the pipeline; modern scikit-learn metadata routing exists precisely for this type of rigor. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn22search16Ņłéturn1search2Ņłéturn4search8Ņłéturn32search1Ņłü

Time-series cross-validation should always mirror deployment. I prefer an expanding or rolling origin split, sometimes with a gap to prevent lag-or-window contamination. If the same user, item, or market appears repeatedly, the split still starts with time, not random entity shuffling; any entity grouping happens inside the time boundary, not instead of it. `TimeSeriesSplit` and rolling-origin evaluation are the right defaults for this reason. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn0search0Ņłéturn18search0Ņłü

For metrics, I separate *predictive* metrics from *decision* metrics. Predictive metrics include log loss and Brier score for purchase probability, calibration curves or reliability diagrams for probabilities, and Poisson or Tweedie deviance for non-negative quantity or revenue. Decision metrics include expected policy value, estimated revenue lift over a baseline, realized average selling price or price floor, action-share compliance, and uncertainty of the offline estimate. A pricing model with decent RMSE but poor calibration by action can still make bad decisions; likewise, a high-AUC purchase model can be useless if it does not rank *actions* correctly for the same context. The meta-lesson across all of this is that staff-level EDA and feature engineering are largely about *avoiding self-deception*. The common failure mode is not that a team forgot to try a deep enough model; it is that the team mistook targeted discounts for causal discounts, let a time leak slip into a rolling feature, or over-trusted a random-split metric in a temporally shifting environment. The strongest workflow is therefore the one that narrows the problem, makes assumptions visible, and turns deployability tests into part of model development rather than a postscript.
